I'm a polyglot developer. I've shipped software in more than forty programming
languages or dialects, and can understand several other languages deeply enough
to port code from those languages. I ported some of the algorithms used in
[diff-lcs][diff-lcs] from [Smalltalk][smalltalk] to [Ruby][ruby], but I don't
count Smalltalk in my known languages (I haven't shipped any software with it).

> **Dialect**: A dialect of a programming language is one that is fairly similar
> to or derived from another programming language. As an example, the Unix
> shells `sh`, `ash`, `bash`, `dash`, `ksh`, and `zsh` are all related to the
> original Bourne shell. They aren't _quite_ the same language -- there are
> features and syntax in `bash` and `zsh` that aren't present in the other
> _dialects_.

## Shipped Languages

> By **shipped**, I mean that I used the language to produce something that I
> shared with others: internal or external at jobs, published as open source, or
> shared with friends or family.

In the language lists below, the presence of † indicates a language which may be
Turing complete but is more considered to be a quasi-programming language. Under
most circumstances, full applications wouldn't or couldn't be written in these
languages -- but there's quite a bit that can still be done with them.
